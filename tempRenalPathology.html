Congenital and Developmental Renal Anomalies
Renal Agenesis
A congenital anomaly where one or both kidneys are absent due to developmental failure.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Developmental failure of the ureteric bud to interact with the metanephric mesenchyme. Sporadic, occasionally familial. Absent kidney(s), empty renal fossae in bilateral cases.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Unilateral cases are often asymptomatic; bilateral leads to oligohydramnios, Potter sequence, usually incompatible with life. Incidence: 1 in 1,000-2,000 (unilateral); rarer for bilateral. Monitoring and management of single kidney function in unilateral; no treatment for bilateral.
Renal Hypoplasia
A congenital condition where kidneys are smaller than normal due to fewer nephrons.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Insufficient nephron development leads to reduced kidney size. Sporadic, can be familial. Small kidneys with normal architecture; no cysts.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Hypertension in unilateral; CKD in bilateral cases. 1 in 1,000-2,000 births, affects both genders. Regular follow-up; diet, medication for CKD.
Renal Ectopia
A congenital condition in which one or both kidneys are located outside their usual anatomical position.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Failure of kidney ascent, possibly due to vascular anomalies. Typically sporadic. Kidney in pelvis or abdomen; sometimes with crossed ectopia.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Often asymptomatic; may cause UTIs, hydronephrosis, or flank pain. 1 in 1,000 births, equal in both sexes. Observation; surgical intervention if obstructive symptoms.
Multicystic Renal Dysplasia
A congenital disorder where a kidney is replaced by multiple non-communicating cysts.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Abnormal renal development, often related to early urinary tract obstruction. Sporadic, usually unilateral. Small, irregular cystic kidney with fibrotic areas; absent normal tissue.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Asymptomatic in unilateral cases; mass may be palpable. 1 in 3,000-5,000 live births. Observation in unilateral; removal if symptomatic or large.
Double and Bifid Ureters
An anomaly where two ureters are present on one side, either separate or partially fused.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Formation of two ureteral buds during development. Typically sporadic, may be familial. Two ureters emerging from a single renal pelvis or partially fused.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Often asymptomatic; may lead to UTIs or hydronephrosis. 1 in 100, relatively common. Observation if asymptomatic; treatment for infections or obstruction.
Cystic Kidney Diseases
Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)
A genetic disorder causing cysts throughout the kidney, leading to progressive kidney failure.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Mutations in PKD1 or PKD2 cause abnormal cell proliferation, fluid secretion. Autosomal dominant; PKD1, PKD2 mutations. Large kidneys with numerous cysts.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Hypertension, hematuria, renal failure by middle age. 1 in 400-1,000, higher in PKD1 mutations. BP control, transplant or dialysis at ESRD.
Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)
A severe genetic disorder causing cystic dilation of renal tubules, often leading to renal failure in childhood.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Mutation in PKHD1 gene affecting fibrocystin protein. Autosomal recessive. Kidneys with fusiform dilated tubules; enlarged kidneys at birth.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Hypertension, renal insufficiency, hepatic fibrosis in severe cases. 1 in 20,000 births. Dialysis, transplant, supportive care for hepatic disease.
Medullary Sponge Kidney
A congenital disorder characterized by cystic dilations of collecting ducts in the kidney medulla.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Developmental defect in renal collecting ducts, exact cause unknown. Sporadic, can be familial. Cystic dilation in medullary and papillary collecting ducts.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Hematuria, recurrent UTIs, nephrolithiasis. Relatively rare, usually detected in adults. Hydration, manage infections; nephrolithiasis treatment as needed.
Nephronophthisis
A genetic disorder characterized by tubulointerstitial fibrosis and progressive kidney failure.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Mutations affecting cilia function and tubular basement membrane. Autosomal recessive; NPHP gene mutations. Fibrosis, small cysts at corticomedullary junction.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Polyuria, polydipsia, progression to ESRD in adolescence. 1 in 50,000 live births. Supportive care, kidney transplant in ESRD.
Simple Renal Cysts
Benign, fluid-filled sacs in the kidneys, often found incidentally in older adults.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Unknown, thought to result from tubular obstruction or cellular proliferation. Sporadic, incidence increases with age. Fluid-filled, thin-walled cysts, typically cortical.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Usually asymptomatic; can cause pain if large or infected. Common in older adults, incidental finding. Observation; drainage or surgery if symptomatic.
Obstructive and Structural Ureteral Anomalies
Ureteropelvic Junction Obstruction
A blockage at the junction of the renal pelvis and ureter, impeding urine flow.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Congenital abnormality in smooth muscle or external compression (crossing vessel). Often sporadic, occasionally familial. Hydronephrosis with thinning renal parenchyma.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Flank pain, UTIs, hydronephrosis, especially in infants. 1 in 1,500 live births, more common in males. Surgical pyeloplasty in symptomatic cases.
Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
A condition where urine flows backward from the bladder into the ureters or kidneys, increasing infection risk.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Primary VUR: congenital due to a defective ureterovesical junction; secondary VUR: due to bladder dysfunction. Often familial in primary cases, autosomal dominant tendency. Reflux of urine; possible renal scarring in chronic cases.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Recurrent UTIs, flank pain, sometimes asymptomatic until infection. 1-3% of children, higher in females. Prophylactic antibiotics, endoscopic injection, or ureteral reimplantation for severe cases.
Renal Diverticulosis
Small outpouchings or diverticula from the renal calyces, often asymptomatic.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Congenital or acquired, often due to increased intrarenal pressure. Typically sporadic. Small sacs protruding from calyces, may have thin walls.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Usually asymptomatic; can lead to UTIs or calculi if urine stasis occurs. Rare, usually incidental finding. Observation; antibiotics if infected; removal if symptomatic or large.
Double and Bifid Ureters
An anomaly where two ureters are present on one side, either separate or partially fused.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Formation of two ureteral buds during development. Typically sporadic, may be familial. Two ureters emerging from a single renal pelvis or partially fused.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Often asymptomatic; may lead to UTIs or hydronephrosis. 1 in 100, relatively common. Observation if asymptomatic; treatment for infections or obstruction.
Bladder and Urethral Abnormalities
Bladder Exstrophy
A congenital anomaly with the bladder exposed outside the abdomen due to lower abdominal wall defect.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Developmental defect in the cloacal membrane during gestation. Sporadic, no specific gene mutation. Visible bladder mucosa outside abdomen.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Continuous urinary leakage, infections, pelvic abnormalities. 1 in 30,000-50,000 births, more common in males. Multistage surgical reconstruction.
Vesical Diverticulosis
Small outpouchings or diverticula in the bladder wall, often due to increased bladder pressure.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Can be congenital or acquired, often secondary to bladder outlet obstruction. Typically sporadic. Pouch-like structures in the bladder wall, usually small.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
UTIs, urinary stasis, hematuria if diverticula become infected. Relatively uncommon, found incidentally. Antibiotics for infection; surgery if symptomatic or obstructive.
Urachal Abnormalities
Congenital anomalies of the urachus, a remnant connecting bladder to umbilicus, may lead to infections or leakage.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Failure of the urachus to close after birth, resulting in patent urachus, cyst, or sinus. Sporadic, non-hereditary. Urachal cysts, sinuses, or patent connection from bladder to umbilicus.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Umbilical discharge, infections, possible hematuria. Rare, often detected in infancy or childhood. Surgical excision for symptomatic cases; antibiotics if infected.
Hypospadias
A congenital defect where the urethral opening is on the underside of the penis rather than at the tip.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Incomplete fusion of urethral folds during development, often due to hormonal disruptions. Familial predisposition; possibly autosomal dominant. Urethral opening on ventral side; chordee may be present in severe cases.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Abnormal urination, ventral curvature of penis (chordee), often detected at birth. 1 in 200-300 male births. Surgical repair typically between 6-18 months of age.
Epispadias
A congenital defect where the urethral opening is on the upper side of the penis, often associated with bladder exstrophy.
PathogenesisInheritanceMorphology
Failure of the genital tubercle and related structures to fuse properly during development. Sporadic, no clear genetic inheritance pattern. Dorsal urethral opening, commonly associated with other pelvic anomalies.
Clinical PresentationEpidemiologyTreatment
Incontinence, abnormal urination, sometimes pelvic and genital abnormalities. Rare, often occurs with bladder exstrophy. Surgical correction, often with bladder reconstruction.
